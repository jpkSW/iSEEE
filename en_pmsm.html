PMSM: PermanentMagnet Synchronous Motor

The synchronous motor with permanent magnets in the rotor is very similar to a DC motor if the excitation winding is replaced by permanent magnets and the stator is exchanged with the rotor.

Experiment with the setting of speed (slider "speed (p.u.)") and torque und Drehmoment (slider "torque (p.u.)")!

In the equivalent circuit diagram, the stator voltage US (green), the voltage drop at the synchronous choke jXS IS (red) and the field e.m.f. UP (blue) are drawn with the same colors as in the voltage phasor diagram.
As long as |n| ≤ 1 it is assumed that the field e.m.f. UP and the stator current IS are always in phase with each other (may be varied with the slider "speed"). In this range, the phasor diagram "inflates" in proportion to the speed. It seems that the stator voltage US is proportional to the speed, as is the field e.m.f. UP.
Why must this be the case in this operating mode?
However, if the machine runs faster than with speed |n| ≥ 1, the phase voltage is no longer sufficient to impress the phase current at the same phase position with an increasing field e.m.f.. Therefore, the phase current must be leading to the field e.m.f.. This is called "active field weakening".
Why does the current have to be leading to the field e.m.f. if the stator voltage US can no longer increase? What would happen if the current were lagging behind the pole wheel voltage?
