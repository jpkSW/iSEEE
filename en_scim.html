SCIM: Squirrel-Cage Induction Machine

The asynchronous machine with squirrel-cage rotor does not require a connection for the rotor winding. The winding consists of metallic bars (aluminum or copper) which are short-circuited with a ring at each end of the rotor.

The sliders are initially set so that an ideal lossless rotor winding is assumed with the rotor stationary.

Here the operation in a grid with constant voltage and constant frequency is considered.

In the equivalent circuit diagram, the stator voltage US (green), the "no-load current" I0 (green), the stator current IS (red) and the rotor current IRσ (blue) are arrowed with the same colors as in the voltage or current pointer diagram.
Since this animation considers operation at constant voltage and frequency, the two "green pointers" US and I0 will not change. Do not change the two sliders (speed = 0, RRΓ = 0) at first and start the animation!
Currents are induced in the short-circuited rotor winding so that the stator field will be almost completely compensated by the rotor field. However, this has the consequence that the north and south poles are exactly opposite each other, so that no torque can be generated.
Up to now the (rotor) winding resistance has been neglected: this is the only reason why the currents IS and IRσ are exactly in phase opposition!
Now increase the rotor winding resistance RRΓ via the lower slider! The greater the resistance, the greater the phase shift between the two currents and thus the north and south poles of the stator and rotor field are pushed apart: the stator poles "push" the rotor poles in front of them!
Now set RRΓ/XσΓ = 0.20 with the lower slider!
Next, vary the speed in the range from 0.00 to 1.00!
At n = 1.00 the rotor has the same speed as the stator field: the rotor "sees" a DC field
=> no voltage is induced into the rotor winding
=> no rotor current flows: the motor has reached the no-load speed
The frequency condition applies here: stator frequency = speed + rotor frequency. Consider the rotor frequency at a speed of 0, 25, 40 and 45 revolutions per second, if a stator frequency of 50 Hz is assumed!
What is then the no-load speed in revolutions per second?
In the just considered speed range of 0...1 the (red) stator poles have "pushed" the (blue) rotor poles in front of them:
=> the machine runs as a motor!
Now increase the speed to values > 1.00! Now it is the other way round: the (blue) rotor poles "push" the (red) stator poles in front of them:
=> the machine runs as a generator!
Now change the direction of rotation of the rotor (n < 0) and the rotor resistance to its largest possible value!
Some compare the stator rotating field and the rotating rotor with two clutch disks rotating with different speed. The state you see now in the animation could be compared to a vehicle still rolling forward, where the reverse gear is engaged...
