DCM: DC-Motor

The buttons can be used to change the direction of rotation (⇒ sign of the voltage) and the direction of torque (⇒ sign of the current).

The DC machine runs as a motor when the induced voltage is less than the armature voltage. Generator operation, on the other hand, occurs when the induced voltage is greater than the armature voltage. Why actually?

The speed is proportional to the induced voltage according to the 1st main equation (Ui = cΦf Ωm). Consequently, if the direction of rotation is changed, the direction of the induced voltage also changes.

The kinetic energy in the drive train is proportional to the square of the speed. Since this stored energy cannot be changed arbitrarily fast, the speed must be the cause and the induced voltage the effect.
The torque is proportional to the armature current according to the 2nd main equation (Mi = cΦf IA). Consequently, if the direction of the current is changed, the direction of the torque also changes.

The armature current (like any current) generates a magnetic field. The field energy stored in this magnetic field is proportional to the square of the armature current (= 1/2 LA IA2). Since this stored energy cannot be changed arbitrarily fast, the armature current must be the cause and the torque the effect.
If the torque acts in the direction of the torque, mechanical power is output: The (DC) machine is operated as a motor in any case. However, if the torque acts in the opposite direction to the direction of rotation, mechanical power is absorbed. If the mechanical power absorbed is greater than the power dissipated in the (DC) machine, electrical power is also output: The (DC) machine is operated as a generator.

However, if the mechanical power absorbed is less than the power loss in the (DC) machine, additional electrical power must be absorbed: The (DC) machine is operated as a brake.

The magnitude and direction of the armature current is ultimately determined by the armature voltage.
